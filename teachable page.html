✨ Accountability & Pen Name Secretary

The lab opened with a lively accountability session as members shared wins, launches, drafting progress, and experiments with AI-powered workflows. The conversation quickly revealed a common thread: authors are no longer struggling to create content—they are struggling to manage the avalanche of it. Between multiple pen names, growing backlists, AI-generated assets, and increasingly complex publishing ecosystems, organization has become the new bottleneck.

To solve this, Kimberly unveiled her evolving “Pen Name Secretary” system: AI-powered orchestrators assigned to individual pen names. Built through Cowork and connected to Supabase and Notion, each secretary can track projects, monitor publishing pipelines, create tasks, run scheduled reports, and even dispatch editing or drafting skills automatically. Rather than one giant publishing system, the goal is an interconnected ecosystem where specialized agents communicate across workflows. The result is a scalable, semi-autonomous creative infrastructure designed to help manage dozens of active brands without losing track of the “glorious stuffy stuff.”

🎼 EZPZ MM Part 3 Tier 5

This section focused on the multimedia expansion of Easy Peasy Marketing Machine, particularly AI-generated songs, image pipelines, and organizational systems for massive marketing output. Kimberly demonstrated how the platform now supports multiple environments—Cowork, Claude Code, Antigravity, Codex, and more—while remaining intentionally platform agnostic. As the amount of generated marketing content grows into the hundreds of assets, new organizational challenges emerge, leading to discussions about dropdown filters, tagging systems, swipe files, and AI-assisted marketing intelligence.

The lab then shifted into AI songwriting. Using Christina’s song pipeline integrated with Suno, Easy Peasy generated thematic songs for Dungeon Daddy Daycare with titles like Radiator Coffee, Dungeon Daddy, and Mr. Frosty’s Truck. Kimberly explored how the AI captured atmosphere and character dynamics surprisingly well, though some songs still lacked emotional specificity. The larger realization was that music generation opens entirely new storytelling and marketing possibilities for authors. Songs become emotional extensions of the books themselves, capable of reinforcing worldbuilding, reader immersion, and audience connection in ways traditional marketing cannot.

🎻 Song Stuffy Stuff

The “Song Stuffy Stuff” section dove deeper into AI-generated instrumental soundtracks and immersive musical storytelling. Kimberly explained that these albums are designed as “reading companions,” intended to emotionally guide listeners through the full arc of a story: arrival, tension, combat, tenderness, crisis, and hope. Using Suno, the system generated motif-threaded soundtrack albums complete with character themes, emotional beats, and ambient worldbuilding tracks tied directly to scenes and narrative tension.

Examples from The Blood Price and Gaze of Stone demonstrated how dramatically different genres could develop distinct musical identities—from Celtic fantasy atmospheres to Greek-inspired cinematic themes. Kimberly emphasized that the process is highly iterative: rerolling prompts, adjusting tone, refining emotional emphasis, and shaping tracks one by one until the soundtrack “feels” right. Beyond immersion, the conversation explored how these albums could become standalone products, ambient audiobook accompaniments, YouTube content, or entirely new revenue streams. The broader insight was that AI music generation is evolving from novelty into a legitimate extension of storytelling craft.

🎬 Book Trailers

This section explored AI-assisted trailer generation for Dungeon Daddy Daycare, using Easy Peasy Marketing Machine to create scripts, shot lists, visual prompts, and storyboard concepts. Kimberly compared the new system to earlier experiments that lacked emotional nuance and leaned too heavily into generic fantasy or horror aesthetics. The updated workflow performed significantly better, generating multiple trailer formats for YouTube, websites, TikTok, and Shorts, complete with soundtrack cues tied directly to the book’s musical themes.

The generated visuals weren’t perfect—there were incorrect character details, odd props, and one unforgettable “dual-wielding battle mop”—but the discussion emphasized that the trailer pipeline is intended as a launch point rather than a final product. The AI provides the scaffolding: emotional beats, cinematic concepts, and visual continuity. Authors can then refine, reroll, or move those prompts into other image or video tools. The larger insight was that AI-generated multimedia is becoming increasingly viable for indie authors, not as replacement art, but as rapid prototyping and creative acceleration.

🖼️ Cover Design

In the cover design segment, Kimberly tested Easy Peasy’s ability to generate full cover concepts for the Nanny of the Apocalypse series. Although the books already have covers, the experiment focused on how AI could rapidly create placeholder concepts, marketing graphics, and visual inspiration. The generated covers captured some of the overall mood and branding—gritty painterly textures, warm amber lighting, post-apocalyptic school settings—but often struggled with story accuracy and character consistency.

The discussion highlighted both the strengths and quirks of modern image models, especially ChatGPT Images 2.0. Kimberly noted that the first generated images often looked stunning, but later generations developed strange muddy textures and mesh-like overlays. Even so, the pipeline demonstrated enormous value as a brainstorming and iteration tool. Easy Peasy successfully created a cohesive visual gallery across the series, offering composition ideas, thematic arcs, and reusable promotional art. The takeaway was clear: AI cover systems may not replace polished final covers yet, but they dramatically reduce the friction of ideation and visual development.

🌌 Wrapup

The closing discussion centered on the growing problem of overwhelm. With AI now capable of generating books, music, trailers, covers, social posts, and entire marketing ecosystems, the challenge is no longer scarcity of content—it is discernment. Participants discussed how creators must increasingly ask strategic questions: What does the audience actually want? Which marketing layers matter most? When should different assets be created? And how do authors avoid drowning in their own possibilities?

This led to the emerging concept of a “Marketing Orchestrator,” an AI layer designed to coordinate all the moving pieces. Rather than running every Easy Peasy module at once, the orchestrator would guide authors through stages of production and marketing based on project needs, timelines, and audience priorities. Kimberly compared the process to building transcontinental railroads: multiple systems developing independently that must eventually connect in the middle. The session closed with optimism that increasingly autonomous AI workflows—combined with thoughtful orchestration—could help authors scale creative businesses without losing themselves in the chaos.
